INDIAN INDIGO, A NARRATIVE OF COLONIAL EXPLOITATION

This story map traces the rise of the indigo industry in Bengal, arguing that economic imperatives led both to the British exploitation of indigenous labor and stymied the development of more efficient systems of indigo cultivation and processing. Research for this project drew upon a wide breadth of secondary literature, drawing from perspectives based in labor-studies, the history of science, agricultural studies and British colonial history, in order to construct a multi-dimensional argument.

Environmental Footprint

In roughly seven days worth of browsing, I downloaded approximately 22272 mb of data. The top 5 sites I trafficked were: 39% www.researchgate.com, 28% mail.google.com, 5% aax-us-east.amazon.adsystem.com, 4% canvas and 24% others. Notably, Storymap is not among my top 5 sites. Truthfully, I did not always use Firefox when working on my Storymap; however, I certainly had the impression that I had used Firefox for Storymap more than anything else. Why then was it absent from the results? As I dug deeper into Carboanalyser’s documentation, I realized that the top 5 sites trafficked correlate not to usage traffic, but to data traffic. Thus, while I may have used Storymap far more than I used www.researchgate.com, www.researchgate.com had a much larger data footprint.

Consequently, my results speak to the environmental impact of remote academic research. Often, scholars and students will utilize online resources in order to ‘limit’ their impact; reading texts online appears more environmentally friendly than printing out 100s of pages or buying a book. While such an assumption may be true, it elides the carbon-footprint of internet usage and, in particular, the large amount of data required to download texts – such as research articles – from the internet. It is also important to consider the impact of the machines we use to do such remote research. In today’s higher-education climate, it is essential to have a functioning laptop; however, the production of laptops is both environmentally deleterious and harmful to humans. Laptops require rare, nonrenewable earth materials whose extraction process produces toxic residue. Moreover, their production and transportation necessitate large amounts of CO2. As our methods of scholarship increasingly more digital – we must ask ourselves, are they truly sustainable? And, if not, how can we continue producing scholarly work in a productive, but also environmentally, friendly manner?
